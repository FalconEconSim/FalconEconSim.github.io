§ 2.1 – 2.4

Indifference Curves

▾

An indifference curve (IC) shows all bundles $(X, Y)$ that give a consumer exactly the same utility. Geometrically, ICs are level curves of the utility function $U(X,Y)$: just as a topographic map shows lines of equal elevation, each IC traces a line of equal satisfaction. The shape of the IC encodes the entire preference structure.

Key Idea
Well-behaved ICs are downward-sloping (both goods have positive marginal utility), convex toward the origin (diminishing marginal rate of substitution), and never intersect (a direct consequence of transitive preferences).
Economic Intuition — Preference Types & the Shape of ICs The shape of the IC encodes everything about preferences. For Cobb-Douglas, α controls the spending share on X: α = 0.7 means 70% of income always goes to X regardless of prices — try it. Perfect substitutes have constant MRS = 1 everywhere, so the consumer is always willing to swap one X for one Y. Perfect complements have ICs that are L-shaped: consuming more of one good without the other adds zero utility (like left and right shoes). Moving to a higher IC always means higher utility — so ICs further from the origin are always preferred.

§ 2.5

The Budget Constraint

▾

The budget constraint $P_X X + P_Y Y = I$ describes all bundles the consumer can afford when spending all income. Its slope $-P_X/P_Y$ is the opportunity cost of X measured in units of Y.

Budget Constraint
$$P_X X + P_Y Y = I \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad Y = \frac{I}{P_Y} - \frac{P_X}{P_Y} X$$
Slope = $-P_X/P_Y$  ·  X-intercept = $I/P_X$  ·  Y-intercept = $I/P_Y$

§ 2.6

Income & Price Changes

▾

An income change produces a parallel shift: both intercepts move proportionally, slope unchanged. A price change produces a pivot: one intercept is fixed, the other moves, and the slope changes.

Common Mistake
Drawing a parallel shift when a price changes. A price change pivots the budget line around one fixed intercept. Only an income change produces a parallel shift.

§ 2.7

The Consumer's Best Choice

▾

The optimal bundle satisfies two conditions simultaneously: budget exhaustion ($P_X X^* + P_Y Y^* = I$) and tangency ($P_X/P_Y = MU_X/MU_Y$). At the tangency point, the market exchange rate equals the consumer's personal trade-off rate.

Two Conditions for Interior Optimum
$$\underbrace{P_X X^* + P_Y Y^* = I}_{\text{budget exhaustion}} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \underbrace{\frac{P_X}{P_Y} = \frac{MU_X}{MU_Y}}_{\text{tangency (MRS = price ratio)}}$$

§ 2.8

Marginal Utility per Dollar

▾

At the optimum, the bang for the buck must be equal across all goods: $MU_X/P_X = MU_Y/P_Y$. If the ratio is higher for X, shift spending toward X — and keep shifting until equality is restored.

Bang-for-the-Buck Condition
$$\frac{MU_X}{P_X} = \frac{MU_Y}{P_Y}$$
This is just the tangency condition rearranged. If $MU_X/P_X > MU_Y/P_Y$, shift spending toward X.

§ 2.9

Cobb-Douglas Demand Functions

For $U(X,Y) = X^\alpha Y^\beta$, the optimal demand functions take a clean closed form. A consumer with Cobb-Douglas preferences always spends a fixed fraction of income on each good, regardless of prices. The income shares $\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)$ and $\beta/(\alpha+\beta)$ are constant.

Cobb-Douglas Demand Functions
$$X^* = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta} \cdot \frac{I}{P_X} \qquad Y^* = \frac{\beta}{\alpha+\beta} \cdot \frac{I}{P_Y}$$
The spending share $\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)$ on good $X$ is constant, independent of income and prices

Economic Intuition — The Income Expansion Path & Engel's Law The purple dashed line in Fig 2.6 is the income expansion path — it traces all the optimal bundles as income grows from 0.5I to I to 1.5I. For Cobb-Douglas preferences, this path is always a straight ray through the origin: as income doubles, both X* and Y* exactly double and the bundle moves proportionally outward. This is what economists call homotheticity. The fixed spending-share property ($\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)$ on X, always) is its signature: no matter how rich or poor the consumer, the fraction of income allocated to each good stays constant. Verify this with the slider: raise Income from 100 to 200 — X* and Y* both double, spending shares stay identical.
